The Infinitely Many Days of Christmas

If after "The Twelve Days of Christmas" the presents kept coming, would we end up with -1/12 presents?

Most of us know the song, but for those who don't, let me summarize it in a mathematician's style. "For 1 ≤ n ≤ 12: On the nth day of Christmas my true love gave to me something (on most days, a bird of some sort)."

Ok now go put all those numbers into your calculator. Just kidding. There's a better way! In elementary school, Karl Friedrich Gauss and his classmated were asked by their teacher to add up all the numbers from 1 to 100. The teacher, surely thinking he had bought himself a few hours, was astounded to find Gauss almost immediately find the answer: 5050! So how did he do this? The number we're looking for is 1 + 2 + ... + 99 + 100. Let's call it S for "sum". Let's do a bit of mathematical gymnastics. S might seem hard to calculate, so let's calculate 2S instead. 2S is simply (100 + 99 + ... + 2 + 1) + (1 + 2 + ... + 99 + 100) We can rearrange this as (1 + 100) + (2 + 99) + ... + (99 + 2) + (100 + 1). Take a second to convince yourself that this is equal to 50×101, which in turn is eaqual to 5050!

Upon me was once in my undergraduate years imparted a piece of valuable advice: "be wise, generalize". So let's take what we've learned and make a theorem out of it. Instead of taking the sum of natural numbers from 1 to 100, let's take the sum of natural numbers from 1 to n. I don't want this post to become a math paper, so I'll give the reader some credit. Let's replace 100 with n in our above equation and obtain 1 + 2 + ... + n = (n/2)(n + 1).

I could end this post here, but that would leave out the folly of the following claim: 1 + 2 + 3 + ... = -1/12. This has been claimed by such sources as Numberphile. But I know what you're thinking. This is surely a ridiculous claim. Clearly the sum should be infinite. How could a sum of positive numbers be negative? How could a sum of integers be a non-integer? Your worries are valid. In fact, I agree with you. Let's destroy their reasoning together.

The argument starts with the claim that 1 - 1 + 1 - 1 + 1 - ... = 1/2. If your instinct is telling you this looks wrong, you're right! But let's give them the benefit of the doubt and look at the proof. I want you to spot the mistake. Let's call this sum X. Now let's compute 1 - X. We have

1 - X = 1 - (1 + 1 - 1 + 1 - ...)

It follows that

1 - X = X

Therefore X = 1/2. So did you find the mistake? Well here it is: We've been treating 1 - 1 + 1 - 1 + 1 - ... as a real number! We called it X and assumed we could perform arithmetic on it. We can't.
